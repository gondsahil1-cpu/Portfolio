A tap-to-log attendance terminal built around RFID authentication, with a live status display and dependable logging — designed to replace manual sign-in sheets in a classroom or lab setting.

Problem Statement

Manual attendance sheets are slow, easy to falsify, and generate no usable data. A classroom needs a fast, tamper-resistant way to log who is present, with immediate feedback so a mis-scan is caught on the spot.

Engineering Solution

An RFID reader authenticates each tag against a known ID list on the microcontroller. A valid scan is timestamped and logged, then confirmed instantly on an LCD/OLED display — invalid or duplicate scans are flagged the same way.

Key Features
  • RFID tag authentication against a stored ID list
  • Real-time attendance logging with timestamps
  • On-device LCD/OLED status feedback for every scan
  • ESP32 (or Arduino) implementation, low part count
  • Deliberately simple, reliable UX — one tap, one result

A differential-drive robot that navigates and solves a maze on its own — reading three ultrasonic sensors, making a turn decision in real time, and avoiding obstacles without any remote input.

Problem Statement

A robot navigating an unknown maze has to make correct turn decisions in real time, with no map and no external control — a wrong call compounds into a wall collision within a few centimeters.

Engineering Solution

Three ultrasonic sensors (front, left, right) feed distance readings into a decision routine on the ESP32, which prioritizes open paths and falls back to a reverse-and-retry when boxed in. Two independently driven wheels give tight, low-radius turns.

Key Features
  • Fully autonomous navigation — no remote control required
  • Three-point ultrasonic obstacle detection (front / left / right)
  • Differential drive for tight, low-radius turning
  • Real-time decision-making algorithm for turn selection
  • Clean wiring loom and deliberate hardware layout

( The Engineer )

I got into embedded systems because software felt incomplete — a program that never touches a wire, a motor, or a sensor is still just a program.

I'm an Electrical Engineering student with a focus on embedded systems, robotics, and automation. I'm drawn to problems that live at the boundary of hardware and software — where a sensor reading has to become a decision, and a decision has to become motion.

My process starts with a real constraint: a wiring limitation, a power budget, a timing requirement. From there I prototype in circuit, iterate in code, and rebuild until the system behaves predictably under real conditions — not just on a breadboard in ideal light.

I care about reliability and clarity as much as functionality: clean wiring, documented pinouts, and code a future engineer — often future me — can actually read.

Field Notes

Writing it down so it stays learned.

Notes from the bench — what actually happened while building, not the polished version.

The hard part of an autonomous maze robot isn't reading a sensor — it's deciding what to do with a reading you don't fully trust. Ultrasonic distance is noisy, corridors reflect sound at odd angles, and a single bad reading turned into a turn command is a wall collision two seconds later.

Three sensors, one decision

Front, left, and right ultrasonic sensors feed a simple priority routine: check front first, then compare left and right when the front is blocked. It sounds trivial written out like that, and the first version was — it also drove straight into corners the moment two readings arrived within a few centimeters of each other.

The fix wasn't code, it was filtering

A short median filter across the last few readings, plus a minimum-confidence threshold before committing to a turn, cleaned up almost every false positive. The lesson generalizes: when an autonomous system misbehaves, the instinct is to rewrite the decision logic — half the time the actual problem is what you're feeding it.

Mechanical tolerance matters as much as firmware

A slightly uneven chassis introduced drift over long runs that no amount of sensor tuning could fully correct. Squaring the wheelbase turned out to matter as much as the decision algorithm — full write-up on the build is in the Works section above.

Most ESP32 problems that look like "the board is broken" are actually power, brownout, or GPIO boot-strapping issues. A short list of the ones that have bitten me more than once:

Power first, always

Driving motors, RFID readers, and Wi-Fi off the same rail as a weak USB supply causes brownout resets that look like random crashes. A dedicated regulated 5V/3.3V rail for the MCU, separate from anything with a motor on it, fixed more "mystery bugs" than any code change.

Boot-strapping pins bite quietly

GPIO0, GPIO2, and a few others have a role during boot — a sensor or LED wired to the wrong pin can silently prevent the board from starting normally, and it's rarely obvious from the symptom alone. Check the boot-strapping table before finalizing a pinout, not after debugging a "dead" board.

The watchdog is a feature, not an annoyance

A blocking loop (a `delay()` inside a sensor poll, a stuck I2C transaction) trips the watchdog and resets the board — which felt like a bug until I realized it was the safety net catching a real hang. Better to design around it than disable it.

An RFID tap looks instant, but there are a few steps happening between the tag entering the reader's field and a row landing in a log — and most of the reliability work lives in the parts you don't see.

Read, then verify — don't trust the first read

The RC522 reads a tag's UID over its own RF field; the reader compares that UID against a known list on the microcontroller rather than trusting anything the tag itself claims to be. That distinction — authenticating against a local list instead of the tag's self-reported identity — is what makes the system tamper-resistant.

Debounce or you'll double-log everyone

A tag held near the reader for even half a second can register as multiple reads. A short debounce window after a valid scan, plus a "same tag, too soon" check, stops a single tap from becoming two attendance entries.

Feedback has to be immediate

The OLED confirmation on every scan — valid or invalid — turned out to matter more than any backend feature. A system that logs silently gets abandoned the first time someone isn't sure their tap registered.

A PID controller reads simple on paper — proportional, integral, derivative, add them up — and then falls apart the first time you tune one against a real motor with real friction and real sensor noise.

Start with P, and expect it to oscillate

Proportional gain alone corrects error fast but overshoots and settles into an oscillation around the target — that's expected, not a failure. It's the baseline you tune the other two terms against, not something to "fix" by cranking it up further.

I kills steady-state error, and it kills stability if you're not careful

Integral gain cleans up the small persistent offset P leaves behind, but it accumulates error over time — a real risk of windup if the system is stuck (a wheel against a wall) with error still being integrated. A clamp on the integral term saved more than one run.

D is the one everyone forgets to filter

Derivative gain damps the oscillation P introduces, but it's also the term most sensitive to sensor noise — an unfiltered derivative on a noisy encoder reading amplifies jitter instead of damping it. A light low-pass filter on the input before differentiating made the difference between smooth and twitchy motion.

Wall following sounds simpler than full maze-solving — keep a wall on one side, maintain a set distance — but it exposes a different set of problems: what "distance" means changes constantly as the wall angle changes, and the algorithm has no memory of the maze's actual shape.

Right-hand rule, until it isn't enough

The simplest version — always keep turning to follow the wall on your right — guarantees you eventually solve a simply-connected maze, but it makes no attempt at an efficient path. It's a good fallback, not a good primary strategy, once the goal shifts from "solve it" to "solve it well."

A single side sensor isn't distance, it's a snapshot

One side-facing ultrasonic reading tells you the distance to whatever's directly across from it right now — it says nothing about whether the wall is approaching, receding, or about to open into a junction. Comparing consecutive readings (is distance increasing or decreasing) turned a static number into something closer to a trajectory.

Junctions are where it actually gets interesting

The front sensor opening up while the side sensor still reads a wall is the signal for an intersection, and that's the moment the maze-solving logic (rather than pure wall-following) has to take over the decision. Getting that handoff clean was most of the actual engineering effort.
